Personal Website

Role: Designer & Developer HTML / CSS JavaScript Figma

This is a personal portfolio site I designed and built completely from scratch. I wanted something that felt clean and professional but still reflected my own style. Everything from the layout to the code was made by me, with no templates or frameworks.

View live site Hosted on GitHub Pages
Visit Site ↗

My Role & Contributions

I handled everything on this project. I came up with the concept, wireframed the pages in Figma, made all the design decisions around layout, typography, and colour, and then coded the whole thing in HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. I also deployed it to GitHub Pages myself.

Design Process

01
Research

I looked at portfolio sites from other UX and front-end designers to understand what worked well. I paid attention to how they organized content and communicated their skills to employers.

02
Wireframes

I wireframed every page in Figma across desktop and mid-size breakpoints before writing any code. This helped me figure out the structure and navigation early on.

03
Prototype & Build

I connected the frames into a clickable prototype to test the flow, then built the final site in code with consistent spacing, type, and colour throughout.

Reflection

Building this site showed me how much design and development inform each other. A lot of decisions I thought were just visual, like spacing and font weight, directly affected how readable the final product felt. If I had more time I would add more motion and interactivity. Overall this project gave me a lot of confidence in my ability to take something from an idea to a deployed, working product on my own.

If I revisited it, I'd push the photography direction further to make the store feel even more distinct.

Baited by the Odds

Role: Researcher & Designer UX Research Print Design Illustrator FigJam

Baited by the Odds is a gambling awareness campaign developed in collaboration with Ottawa Public Health as part of the MTM6600 Applied Project. Our team designed a system of campaign assets — including a digital poster and 15-second motion graphic — to help athletes and coaches recognize the risks of problem gambling in sport culture.

My Role & Contributions

I contributed across the full UX research phase — building out the empathy map, participating in the iceberg model analysis, and contributing to the ideation sprint. On the design side, I developed poster layout concepts and worked on the visual identity of the campaign to make it feel credible and non-judgmental for the target audience.

Design Process

01
Research

We mapped the problem space using an empathy map and iceberg model to understand what athletes and coaches hear, see, think, and feel about gambling — and why it goes unaddressed.

02
Ideation

The team ran a broad ideation sprint across four categories: video/digital media, games, education, and physical items. We used a virtual whiteboard to cluster and evaluate concepts before narrowing to a digital-first campaign.

03
Design & Iteration

We sketched multiple poster directions, refined typography and layout through several iterations, and settled on a bold orange palette with a fishing hook metaphor to represent how "one small bet can reel you in."

Empathy Map — Understanding the Athlete & Coach
Iceberg Model — Mapping Systemic Causes of Gambling in Sport
Ideation Sprint — Four Categories of Campaign Concepts
Sketch Iterations — Poster Layout & Messaging Exploration
High-Fidelity Refinements — Final Poster Direction

Reflection

This was my first project designed for a real external client. Working within Ottawa Public Health's guidelines meant every design decision had to serve a genuine public health goal, not just look good. The research phase was more intensive than any of my solo projects — the empathy map and iceberg model forced our team to think beyond surface-level symptoms and understand the systemic reasons gambling goes unaddressed in sport. It also pushed me to design for accessibility and clarity above all else, which is something I'm carrying into every project going forward.

EchoLink — Mobile App

Role: UX Designer & Researcher Figma FigJam UX Research Prototyping

EchoLink is an AI-powered silent communication app that lets users communicate through lip reading and gesture recognition — no voice required. Designed as part of a group UX project, EchoLink targets people in loud environments, deaf users, and anyone who needs to communicate discreetly. I contributed to the full UX process from research through high-fidelity prototype.

My Role & Contributions

I worked across the full project — participating in the focus group research, contributing to the FigJam ideation board, developing user personas and journey maps, and designing high-fidelity screens in Figma. I also helped build the user flow diagrams and app flow structure that shaped the final prototype.

Design Process

01
Research & Focus Group

We ran a focus group to gather real feedback on the concept — asking people about silent communication, trust in AI lip-reading, and gesture usability. Responses shaped our feature priorities.

02
Personas & Journey Maps

We built three user personas — Sarah (a college student), Carlos (a factory worker), and Jake (a nightlife attendee) — each with full journey maps covering stages, goals, pain points, and emotions.

03
App Flow & User Flows

We mapped the full app structure and designed three detailed user flows: AI-Powered Lip-Reading Mode, Gesture Messaging, and Custom Gesture Creation — covering every decision point and error state.

04
Hi-Fi Prototype

The final screens were built in Figma with a dark, tech-forward aesthetic. Key screens include onboarding, permissions, settings, practice mode, and the core messaging interface.

FigJam — Research Board, Problem Statement & Ideation
App Flow — Information Architecture
User Flows — Lip Reading, Gesture Messaging & Custom Gesture Creation
User Journey Maps — Sarah, Carlos & Jake

Reflection

EchoLink was the most research-intensive project I've worked on. Having to design for three very different users — a student, a factory worker, and a nightclub attendee — forced me to constantly check my assumptions against real user needs rather than defaulting to what looked good. The focus group feedback was humbling; things I assumed were intuitive weren't. Building out the full user flows also gave me a much clearer picture of how many edge cases a real product has to account for, and how early decisions in the flow cascade into the entire experience.
